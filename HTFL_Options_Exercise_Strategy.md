**Two types of options matter differently for taxes:**

## The Four Scenarios

### Scenario A: Exercise Everything at Departure — Then Retire
❌ **Do not do this.**

When you exercise all remaining ISO options at once at departure (at $30/share), you create **$833,000 in new AMT preference income**. This generates a new AMT bill of approximately **$228,000**.

The problem: in retirement with no salary, your regular income tax (paid at low LTCG rates on investment income) is roughly equal to or below your AMT threshold. **You can never recover the $228,000 credit.** It's effectively a permanent tax that you pay and never see again.

> **Bottom line:** Exercising all ISOs in a lump sum at retirement loses you ~$228,000 permanently. Avoid this.

---

### Scenario B: Exercise Everything at Departure — Then Get a New Job
✅ **Viable, but requires planning.**

Same as Scenario A — you exercise all ISOs within 90 days of leaving, creating a $228,000 AMT bill. But with a new job providing salary income, your regular income tax rises enough to recover the AMT credit.

**Recovery speed depends on your new salary:**

| New Job Salary | Annual AMT Credit Recovery | Years to Recover |
|---------------|---------------------------:|----------------:|
| $150,000 | $16,228 | 14 years |
| $300,000 | $50,494 | 4.5 years |
| $462,500 | $64,287 | 3.6 years |

The recovery also speeds up as you sell HeartFlow shares, because selling ISO shares generates a special tax adjustment that reduces your AMT further (see Scenario B2 below).

> **Bottom line:** Works if you're confident you'll land a $300K+ job quickly. Risky if your next role takes time to find or pays less.

### Scenario B2: Exercise Everything at Departure — Then Retire, But Sell ISO Shares
✅ **This is your key insight — and it works.**

Here's the elegant part: **the act of selling the newly exercised ISO shares for diversification is itself the AMT recovery mechanism**, even without a new job.

**Here's why it works:**

When you exercise an ISO at $30 (setting your AMT "cost basis" at $30), and then sell that share at $30 a year later, something special happens:

- For regular tax: your gain is $30 − $2.22 = **$27.78/share** → you pay normal capital gains tax on this
- For AMT: your gain is $30 − $30 = **$0/share** → AMT sees no gain at all
- This creates a **negative AMT adjustment of $27.78/share** that wipes out your AMTI and drops your tentative minimum tax near zero
- With a near-zero tentative minimum tax, your regular capital gains tax now exceeds the AMT → you recover the credit

**Critical detail:** The $27.78 negative adjustment per share is **permanently fixed** at the moment you exercise. It doesn't change whether the stock later rises to $50 or falls to $20. You always get the same credit back.

**Recovery schedule in retirement (no new job, $30 stock price):**

| Year | ISO Lot You Sell | Negative AMT Adjustment | AMT Credit Recovered | Remaining Balance |
|------|-----------------|------------------------:|---------------------:|------------------:|
| 2031 | $2.22 grant (7,865 shares) | -$218,490 | $49,273 | $179,223 |
| 2032 | $2.72 grant (17,123 shares) | -$467,115 | $97,127 | $82,096 |
| 2033 | $8.33 ISO grant (6,813 shares) | -$147,638 | $38,646 | $43,450 |
| 2034 | Residual | — | $43,450 | **$0** |

**Full $228,000 AMT credit recovered in 3–4 years, in retirement, without a new job.**

The key: you're already planning to sell HeartFlow shares to diversify. That planned diversification also recovers your AMT. You're not doing extra work — the two goals align perfectly.

> **Conditions that must be met:**
> 1. Exercise ISO options within 90 days of leaving HeartFlow
> 2. Have $405,000 in cash ready immediately
> 3. Hold the newly exercised shares for at least **12 months** before selling
> 4. Then sell them gradually as part of your diversification plan

## The 90-Day Clock — Specific Risks

If you choose Scenario B2 (exercise at departure, retire + sell), here are the precise failure modes to watch for:

### Risk 1: Missing the Window *(Most Severe)*
If you don't exercise ISOs within 90 days of your last day at HeartFlow, they automatically convert to Non-Qualified Options (NSOs). The $685K spread that would have been recoverable AMT becomes **permanent ordinary income taxed at ~45%** — an extra $149,000 in taxes you can never recover.

**Fix:** Exercise on Day 1 of departure. Start the Fidelity process the week you give notice.

---

### Risk 2: Grant Expires Before the Window Closes *(High)*
⚠️ **This one is specific to your situation.**

The **1,712-share $8.33 NSO grant expires on September 20, 2029.** If you leave HeartFlow after **July 22, 2029**, the 90-day window runs past the expiration date — and these options expire worthless before you can exercise them. That's $37,099 of value gone.

**Fix:** Either leave before July 22, 2029 — or exercise this specific grant before your departure date (you can exercise vested options while still employed).

The **721-share and 31-share grants expire April 22, 2030** — giving you more time but still worth tracking.

---

### Risk 3: Cash Not Ready *(High)*
You need **$405,235 in cash** available within 90 days of departure. This is not the kind of money you want to scramble for during a life transition.

**Fix:** Before your planned departure, move $405K into a liquid savings account and leave it untouched. Think of it as a reserved "option exercise fund."

---

### Risk 4: Selling Too Soon *(High)*
After exercising ISOs at departure, you must hold the shares for **at least 12 months** before selling. If you sell earlier — even to cover retirement expenses — it becomes a "disqualifying disposition," which means:
- The profit is reclassified as ordinary income (taxed at ~45%)
- The negative AMT adjustment disappears
- Your $228K AMT credit is never recovered

This is the most insidious risk because the temptation is real. In the first year of retirement with no salary, if cash gets tight, the newly exercised stock sitting in your account can feel like an obvious source of funds. Tapping it would be very costly.

**Fix:** Before exercising, make sure you have at least **12 months of living expenses** in separate, liquid accounts that you would never need to touch. The exercised shares are off-limits for one full year.

---

### Risk 5: Stock Price at Exercise *(Medium)*
Whatever HeartFlow is trading at when you exercise sets your AMT cost basis — and your AMT bill:

| HTFL Price at Exercise | AMT Bill | Cash Needed (exercise + AMT) |
|------------------------|--------:|-----------------------------:|
| $20 | ~$139,000 | ~$544,000 |
| $30 | ~$228,000 | ~$633,000 |
| $40 | ~$311,000 | ~$716,000 |
| $60 | ~$483,000 | ~$888,000 |

Higher prices mean a bigger AMT bill, but also larger negative adjustments when you sell — so recovery is faster. The real risk is the **cash flow shock** at departure, not the long-term tax outcome.

**Fix:** If the stock has risen dramatically before you plan to leave, consider Scenario C (phased exercise while employed) to avoid the cash crunch.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Never exercise all ISOs at departure and simply retire** without a plan to sell the shares. The AMT credit is permanently lost.

2. **Selling HeartFlow shares for diversification IS the AMT recovery.** The two goals reinforce each other — you don't need a new job to recover AMT if you were planning to sell anyway.

3. **The 90-day clock is the highest-risk element** of any "exercise at departure" strategy. Prepare the cash in advance and exercise immediately on day one.

4. **One grant expires September 2029** — check your departure date carefully against this.

5. **Exercising gradually while still employed** (Scenario C) eliminates all of these risks and is the optimal strategy if you have 2–3 more years at HeartFlow.
